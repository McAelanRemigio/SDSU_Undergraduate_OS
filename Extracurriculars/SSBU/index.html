Overview

Competitive gaming has been one of the longest-running commitments of my life. What started as a hobby evolved into a long-term practice in competition, community building, event organization, commentary, and historical documentation.


Across multiple platform fighter communities, I have contributed as a competitor, crew leader, commentator, organizer, and archivist. Over time, esports became less about individual matches and more about understanding systems: how communities form, how players improve, and how competitive environments sustain themselves.

What I Built

Competitive Career

I have competed across grassroots and collegiate scenes in multiple platform fighters including Super Smash Bros. Ultimate, Super Smash Flash 2, and Flash Party.

  • Ranked #1 overall in the Scripps Ranch Power League
  • Winner of ARSC Smash Strike 2020
  • Qualified for SSF2 Oasis Invitational
  • Represented Aztec Esports at San Diego State University

Community Leadership

Beyond competition, I contributed to grassroots organization and community building efforts, including founding crews, supporting tournament operations, and mentoring newer players.

  • Founded “Peacekeepers” crew in early Flash Party scene
  • Supported local tournament operations and event logistics
  • Mentored newer competitors in regional communities

Commentary & Broadcasting

I provided live commentary for community tournaments, developing skills in real-time analysis, audience communication, and competitive storytelling.

Outcomes

  • 6+ years of active participation in competitive gaming communities
  • Multiple top placements across grassroots tournaments
  • Collegiate representation through Aztec Esports
  • Community-building contributions across multiple scenes
  • Maintained public competitive history archive

What I Learned

Competitive gaming taught me how to improve through failure. Tournament environments provide immediate feedback loops where growth requires honest self-assessment and iteration.


Just as importantly, it showed me that communities are not automatic — they are built by people willing to invest time and structure into sustaining them.


Many of the leadership skills I later applied in academic and professional settings were first developed in these grassroots environments.
