Why I Started DSI

I founded the Data Science Initiative during my freshman year at SDSU because there was no centralized community for students interested in data science. What existed was fragmented — interest without structure, curiosity without a home.


DSI began as a simple idea: build a real community around applied data science. Not just meetings or networking, but a space where students could actually develop skills, build projects, and gain meaningful experience together.

What I Built

1. A Structured Student Community

  • Scaled SDSU’s first data science organization to 160+ members
  • Built founding leadership structure and early organizational systems
  • Focused on depth of engagement over passive membership growth

2. Applied Learning Curriculum

  • Designed a year-long skill-building roadmap for data science fundamentals
  • Structured progression from foundational concepts → applied work
  • Emphasized competence and project readiness over theory alone

3. Student Consulting Arm

  • Created DSI Consulting to deliver real projects for campus units and startups
  • Introduced project lead + deliverable-based team structures
  • Helped students translate academic learning into portfolio work

Outcomes

  • Built and scaled a 160+ member student organization
  • Supported 15–20+ students in securing internships through applied project experience
  • Established partnerships with campus units and external stakeholders
  • Created structured pipelines from learning → applied work → professional outcomes

Leadership Decisions & Tradeoffs

DSI was built without waiting for institutional permission. The goal was to create something functional and real from the start, even if it meant learning publicly.


Key decisions included prioritizing structured programming over informal community, emphasizing applied outcomes over participation, and building systems early to avoid long-term organizational fragility.

What I Learned

Building DSI taught me that leadership is not only vision — it is systems, people, and sustainability.


Early intensity created momentum, but also revealed the importance of delegation, scope control, and emotional sustainability in long-term organizational growth.


The most important realization: impact and sustainability are not the same thing.
